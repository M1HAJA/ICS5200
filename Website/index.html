The Role of Artificial Intelligence in Cyberbullying on Social Media

A dual-approach study investigating how AI both amplifies and mitigates cyberbullying within Malta's unique digital environment — integrating socio-technical research with a proof-of-concept Maltese-language detection model.

Author: Jan Lawrence Formosa Supervisor: Prof. Vanessa Camilleri Department: Artificial Intelligence, Faculty of ICT

About This Research

What is this dissertation?

A socio-technical study examining the dual role of AI in cyberbullying within the Maltese context. It combines a systematic literature review, PESTLE-based horizon scanning, and expert interviews with the development and evaluation of a BERT-based cyberbullying detection model trained on Maltese-language data.

Why does it matter?

Malta's small, tightly interconnected population amplifies the psychological and reputational consequences of online harm. This research contributes a locally grounded framework for responsible AI governance, policy development, and the design of safer digital environments.

Research Objectives

Systematic Literature Review

A PRISMA-guided review across ACM, IEEE Xplore, ProQuest, and EBSCO — identifying trends in AI-assisted cyberbullying, detection architectures, and ethical governance.

Horizon Scanning

PESTLE-structured foresight analysis of the SLR corpus, surfacing emerging technological, social, legal, and regulatory signals relevant to AI-driven online harm.

Expert Interviews

Semi-structured interviews with professionals from academic, regulatory, and industry backgrounds, contextualising findings within Malta's digital ecosystem.

Detection Model

A fine-tuned BERTu transformer model trained on Maltese-language sentiment data, demonstrating the feasibility of automated cyberbullying detection.

Malta-Centric Framework

A structured synthesis of research and technical findings offering actionable, locally relevant considerations for AI-driven cyberbullying mitigation.

Policy Contribution

Bridging the gap between AI misuse and mitigation, informing responsible deployment and supporting Malta's emerging national AI governance landscape.

Key Findings

AI as a Dual-Use Technology

AI simultaneously amplifies and mitigates cyberbullying. Generative models, deepfakes, and automated bots have lowered barriers to harassment at scale, while transformer-based detection systems offer scalable moderation solutions. AI's impact depends entirely on how it is designed, deployed, and governed.

Malta's Unique Context

Malta's small, closely interconnected population intensifies the consequences of online harm. Local data shows 24.5% of Maltese adolescents aged 13–16 experienced cyberbullying, and 79% of Maltese respondents expressed high concern about AI-generated fake content — well above the EU average of 68%.

BERTu Detection Model Results

The fine-tuned BERTu model achieved a macro F1 score of 0.867, outperforming the multilingual mBERTu model (0.839). The Maltese-specific model showed stronger performance and stability. Both models struggled with sarcasm and irony — a key limitation for real-world deployment.
